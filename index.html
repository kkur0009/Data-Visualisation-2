World Population in 2020

This visualisation caters to researchers, policymakers, and the general public. Researchers can explore population trends and societal impacts, while policymakers can make informed decisions. It also serves as an educational resource for students, educators, and journalists, offering insights into global population dynamics based on various variables. The data is based on the world population in 2020.

Population by Country

The map illustrates the global population distribution by country for the year 2020. Using a gradient from light yellow to dark green to represent population sizes, countries like China and India stand out with the darkest shades, indicating the highest populations. Conversely, regions like Canada, Australia, and parts

of North Africa exhibit lighter colors, signifying lower populations. The population density in regions like Western Africa, the USA, and Brazil is moderate, while Europe displays a relatively even distribution. The map offers zoom controls and centering options for more detailed exploration of specific regions.

Fertility Rate vs Migrantions

The bubble chart depicts the relationship between a country's fertility rate and its number of migrants for the year 2020. Bubbles are colour-coded based on continents and sized according to the population of the respective country. Most countries cluster between 1 and 3 in fertility rate, showing little net migration (close to zero). The United States stands out with a higher number of migrants and a fertility rate near 2.

Conversely, Venezuela exhibits a significant negative migration but also falls within the same fertility range. The size of the bubbles indicates that densely populated nations like those in Asia and Africa tend to have higher fertility rates. The option to filter by continent and minimum population offers users the flexibility to narrow down the analysis to specific regions or countries of interest.

Urban Population

The radial chart showcases the urban population change by continent. Asia, represented by the largest segment in green, indicates a significant urban population growth or size compared to other continents. Africa, though smaller than Asia, still occupies a noteworthy portion, suggesting a substantial urban population shift. Europe, North America, and Oceania have smaller segments, implying less dramatic urban population changes in these regions. South America's contribution is the smallest, hinting at minimal urban population variation. The chart also provides an option to adjust for minimum population, which may further refine the data representation. Overall, the most pronounced urban population changes or sizes seem to be occurring in Asia and Africa.

Change In Population

Average Age

The double donut chart vividly illustrates the change in population across continents, with each concentric circle potentially representing different metrics or timeframes. Dominating both donuts, Asia's segment showcases the most substantial population dynamics, closely followed by Africa. Although the outer donut positions Africa's change as less significant compared to Asia, the inner ring suggests a rising trend for Africa, narrowing the gap. Europe, North America, Oceania , and South America, portrayed in hues which occupy smaller portions in both rings. Notably, variations between the two rings highlight different population metrics or trends for these continents.

This dual-donut visualisation effectively highlights the multifaceted nature of global population shifts, providing a comprehensive view of both absolute and relative changes across continents.

The graph depicts the median age by continent, represented through horizontal bars of varying lengths. Europe stands out with the highest median age, indicated by an average of 41.95 years of age , suggesting an older population and lower birth rates. North America, follows, indicating an aging population but slightly younger than Europe. Oceania, has a median age somewhere in between Asia and North America. Asia and South America, exhibit younger median ages, possibly due to higher birth rates or a younger population in general. Africa, has the lowest median age, alluding to a predominantly younger population.

Overall, the graph provides a clear snapshot of the age distribution across continents, highlighting the aging trends in Europe and North America, and the youth-centric demographic in Africa.
